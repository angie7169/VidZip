How to Compress a Video on iPhone (2026)

Updated July 2026 · 9 min read · By VidZip Team

iPhone videos look fantastic — and they eat storage like nothing else. A single minute of 4K HDR footage from a recent iPhone can run 400 MB or more. Whether you're trying to free up space, fit a clip under an email or messaging limit, or just share something without it choking on a slow connection, you need to compress it. This guide walks through every method available on iPhone in 2026, compares them honestly, and tells you which one to actually use.

Method 1: Change Camera Settings (Prevention, Not Cure)

The cheapest fix is to shoot smaller videos in the first place. Open Settings → Camera → Record Video and pick a lower setting. "1080p at 30 fps" instead of "4K at 60 fps" cuts file size by roughly 80% before you even press record.

The catch: this only affects future recordings. It does nothing for the 200 MB clip you already shot and need to email right now. For existing videos, you need one of the methods below.

Best for: Setting and forgetting, so next time the problem doesn't happen.

Method 2: The Shortcuts App (Free, Fiddly)

iOS doesn't have a "compress video" button in Photos, but the built-in Shortcuts app can do it. You either find a shared "Compress Video" shortcut online or build one yourself using the "Encode Media" action.

The process: add the shortcut, run it, pick the video, pick a size, wait. It works, and it's free. But there are real downsides:

  • It downscales resolution. "Encode Media" asks for a target size in pixels, not bitrate. Your 4K video becomes 720p whether that's what you wanted or not.
  • No target file size. You can't say "make this 25 MB." You're guessing at resolution and hoping the result fits.
  • Permissions friction. The first time you run a shortcut that touches Photos, iOS makes you grant access step by step.
  • One video at a time. No batch processing.

Best for: One-off compressions where you don't mind losing resolution and don't want to install anything.

Method 3: iMovie (Free, Slow, Heavy-Handed)

Apple's iMovie can export a video at lower quality. Import the clip into a project, tap Done, then tap the share icon and pick Export Project at a smaller resolution like 720p or 540p.

iMovie works, but it's a video editor pretending to be a compressor, and it shows:

  • Slow export. iMovie re-encodes the entire project timeline, even for a single clip with no edits.
  • Resolution-only control. You pick 720p or 540p — you can't fine-tune bitrate or set a target file size.
  • Heavy downscaling. The quality drop from 4K to 540p is dramatic and immediately visible.
  • Clunky for batches. Each video needs its own project. Compressing ten clips means ten exports.

Best for: When you already have iMovie installed, only need one video, and don't mind a coarse quality drop.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best way to compress a video on iPhone?

Use a purpose-built compressor app like VidZip. It keeps your original resolution, lets you set a target file size, and works fully offline. iOS Settings only affect future recordings, Shortcuts is fiddly and downscales, and iMovie re-encodes slowly. For real control over size and quality, a dedicated compressor is the practical choice.

Can I compress a video on iPhone without an app?

Not directly. iOS Photos has no compress button. You can build a Shortcut that re-encodes at lower resolution, but it reduces quality more than necessary and requires setup. The only built-in options that meaningfully shrink an existing video involve either Shortcuts or iMovie.

How much can I compress an iPhone video?

Typically 70-85% smaller with no visible quality loss using a Balanced or Space Saver preset. A 200 MB clip can become 30-60 MB. Pushing past 85% smaller starts to introduce visible blocky artifacts, especially in dark or high-motion scenes.

Does compressing a video reduce quality?

Technically yes, but with careful bitrate control the difference is invisible on a phone screen. The key is lowering bitrate while keeping resolution. Methods that downscale resolution (like iMovie or most Shortcuts) damage quality far more visibly than bitrate-only compression.

Where do compressed videos save on iPhone?

With VidZip, tap Save to Photos on the result screen to write the compressed video back to your Photos library, or Save to Files to choose a folder. If you leave the result screen without saving, the temporary file is deleted to free up space.
