HEVC (H.265) vs H.264: Which Is Better?

Updated July 2026 · 9 min read · By VidZip Team

If you've ever wondered why your iPhone videos won't play on a Windows PC, or why some files are half the size of others at the same quality, the answer is almost always the codec — specifically, HEVC (H.265) vs H.264. This comparison breaks down the real differences in size, quality, compatibility, and licensing, and tells you which one to use when.

The Short Answer

HEVC (H.265) is the modern, efficient codec: same quality at about half the file size of H.264. H.264 is the universal, compatible codec: plays everywhere, but files are bigger. Pick HEVC when you control playback and care about size; pick H.264 when you need guaranteed compatibility.

iPhone shoots HEVC by default to save storage. That's great until you send a video to a Windows friend and they can't open it. Then you need to convert. Converting HEVC to MP4 (H.264) is the common fix.

Quality: Can You See the Difference?

At matched bitrate, HEVC looks better — that's the whole point. More interesting is the question: if you have an H.264 file and an HEVC file at the same perceived quality, which looks better? The honest answer: at normal viewing distances on a phone or laptop, neither. They look the same. The HEVC file is just smaller.

You only see differences in edge cases: very low bitrate (HEVC handles it more gracefully), heavy film grain (H.264 sometimes retains texture better), and fast motion with fine detail (HEVC's block management can be cleaner). For 99% of consumer video, "HEVC = same quality at half size" is accurate.

Compatibility: The Real Reason This Matters

This is where HEVC loses. The codec world is fragmented by licensing — HEVC patents are owned by multiple pools, and browser makers (Chrome, Firefox) refuse to pay. Result:

  • Plays HEVC natively: Apple devices, recent Android, Safari, modern Windows (with paid extension), some smart TVs.
  • Doesn't play HEVC: Chrome and Firefox (in most cases), older Windows without the extension, many budget Android devices, older smart TVs, most projectors and conference room gear.
  • Always plays H.264: Everything made in the last 15 years.

If you're sharing video and don't know what the recipient's setup is, H.264 is the safe bet. That's why VidZip outputs H.264 MP4 by default — universal playback is more valuable than incremental efficiency for shared videos.

When to Use Each

Use HEVC when:

  • You're keeping videos on your iPhone/Mac for personal viewing.
  • Storage is tight and you need every MB to count.
  • You're archiving raw footage for later editing.
  • All viewers are in Apple's ecosystem.

Use H.264 when:

  • You're sharing to Windows, Android, or unknown devices.
  • You're uploading to a website or sending by email/messaging.
  • You're editing in older or pickier software.
  • Compatibility matters more than file size.

What About AV1 and the Future?

Both H.264 and HEVC will eventually be eclipsed by AV1 — a royalty-free codec from the Alliance for Open Media that's another 30% more efficient than HEVC. AV1 is already supported in modern Chrome, Firefox, and newer hardware. But AV1 encoding is still slow on phones, and it'll be years before it replaces HEVC for capture. For now, the H.264/HEVC pair covers almost all real-world cases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is HEVC better than H.264?

For quality-per-bit, yes. HEVC (H.265) is roughly 40-50% more efficient than H.264, meaning the same visual quality at about half the file size. The tradeoff is compatibility and licensing — HEVC isn't supported everywhere and codec licensing is more expensive for software makers.

Can H.264 devices play HEVC?

Not without help. Apple devices, modern Android, and recent Windows 10/11 can play HEVC natively (Windows requires a paid extension from the Microsoft Store). Older Windows, many web browsers (only Safari plays HEVC), and legacy hardware cannot. If you need universal playback, convert HEVC to H.264 MP4.

How much smaller is HEVC than H.264?

About 40-50% smaller at the same perceived quality. A 100 MB H.264 clip can become roughly 50-60 MB in HEVC with no visible quality drop. The exact savings depend on resolution, motion, and how aggressively you encode.

Why does iPhone default to HEVC?

Because iPhone storage is precious and HEVC halves file size. Apple controls both the capture and playback sides of its ecosystem, so HEVC's compatibility problem doesn't bite within Apple's world. Issues only show up when sharing to Windows, browsers, or older devices.

Should I shoot in HEVC or H.264?

Shoot in HEVC (iPhone's default) for storage efficiency, then convert to H.264 only when sharing outside Apple's ecosystem. If you constantly share with Windows users and hate converting, switch iPhone Camera settings to Most Compatible (H.264) — files will be bigger but play everywhere.
