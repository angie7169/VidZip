How to Batch Compress Videos on iPhone

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read · By VidZip Team

A single iPhone video can be hundreds of megabytes. Now multiply that by a vacation, a wedding, or a kid's birthday — and you're staring at 10, 50, or 200 gigabytes of footage eating your storage. Compressing them one by one is soul-crushing. Batch compression is the answer: queue up everything at once, walk away, come back to a phone with gigabytes of free space. This guide shows how.

Why Batch Compression Matters

The arithmetic is brutal. A one-week trip with daily 4K clips can easily produce 50 GB of video. At one video per compression session, you'd spend hours tapping through menus. At 30 seconds per manual compression — pick video, choose preset, wait, save, repeat — 100 videos is nearly an hour of babysitting your phone.

Batch compression collapses that into "select all, tap once, come back later." The app handles the queue; you handle your life. For anyone with a photo library that's slipped out of control, this is the single feature that makes cleanup realistic.

What iPhone's Built-in Tools Can Do (and Can't)

Apple's default apps don't help here:

  • Photos. No compression at all. You can delete, trim, or apply filters, but there's no "make this smaller" button.
  • iMovie. One project, one video at a time. You can technically string clips into a project and export at lower quality, but that merges them into one file — not what you want.
  • Shortcuts. You can build a shortcut that loops over multiple videos and re-encodes each one, but it's slow, error-prone, and downscales resolution (see our compress video on iPhone guide for why that's bad).

None of these handle batches the way you actually want: select a bunch, compress each one as a separate file, save them all back. For that, you need a dedicated compressor.

Step-by-Step: Batch Compression With VidZip

  1. Open VidZip and tap Photos or Files.
  2. Select multiple videos — tap each one you want, or drag to select a range. There's no limit in Pro.
  3. On the Compress screen, you'll see a thumbnail grid of every selected video with its size and duration.
  4. Pick a quality preset (the same setting applies to every video in the batch). Balanced is the sweet spot: ~70% smaller with no visible quality loss.
  5. Tap Compress. VidZip shows a progress ring plus a per-video queue, so you can watch each clip compress in turn.
  6. When the batch finishes, tap Save to Photos to write all results back to your library at once. The originals stay untouched.

Free users can batch up to 2 videos per compression; Pro unlocks unlimited batch size. For a trip's worth of footage, Pro pays for itself the first time you use it.

Tips for Big Batches

  • Plug in your phone. Large batches burn battery. Keep it charged while compressing.
  • Free up working space first. VidZip creates temporary copies during compression; very large batches on a nearly-full phone can fail. Clear a few GB of headroom first (you can clear VidZip's working folder from Settings → Storage).
  • Use Balanced, not Space Saver, for keepsakes. Space Saver's extra savings introduce mild artifacts you'll notice on videos you rewatch. Balanced is invisible.
  • Save to Files for archives. If you're compressing to move footage off your phone, save the batch to Files → iCloud Drive or an external location instead of back to Photos.
  • Preview before deleting originals. Always check a few compressed clips before you delete the originals. Compression rarely fails, but it's one-way — once the original is gone, it's gone.

When to Batch vs. Compress One at a Time

Batch is for quantity. One-at-a-time still wins in a few cases:

  • You need different settings per video. Batch applies one preset to all. If clip A needs Target Size mode for email and clip B needs High Quality for an archive, do them separately.
  • You want to fine-tune quality. Batch is set-and-forget. For a single hero clip you care about, compressing individually lets you compare presets.
  • You're sharing immediately. Compress the one clip you need to send right now; batch the rest later.

For cleanup, archive, and "reclaim my storage" use cases, batch wins every time.

Yes, with the right app. Apple's built-in tools (Photos, iMovie) only handle one video at a time. VidZip and other dedicated compressors support batch mode — select any number of videos, pick a preset, and they all compress in one pass.

How long does batch compression take?

Each video compresses in a few seconds, so batch time scales roughly linearly with total video length. 10 short clips might take under a minute; 50 long clips could take several minutes. You can queue the batch and walk away — VidZip processes them one after another automatically.

Does batch compression lower quality more than single?

No. Each video in the batch is compressed with the same settings as if it were the only one. Batch mode is just automation — it runs the same compression on each file sequentially. Quality is identical to compressing one at a time.
