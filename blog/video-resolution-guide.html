Video Resolution, Framerate & Bitrate

Updated July 2026 · 9 min read · By VidZip Team

Every video is defined by three numbers: resolution (how many pixels), framerate (how many frames per second), and bitrate (how much data per second). Together they determine what the video looks like and how big the file is. Marketing pushes resolution (4K! 8K!), but the truth is more interesting — and once you understand how the three interact, you'll make much better decisions about shooting, sharing, and compressing video.

Resolution: How Many Pixels

Resolution is the pixel dimensions of each frame. Common ones:

Name Pixels Megapixels
720p (HD)1280 × 7200.9 MP
1080p (Full HD)1920 × 10802.1 MP
1440p (2K/QHD)2560 × 14403.7 MP
4K (UHD)3840 × 21608.3 MP
8K7680 × 432033 MP

More pixels means finer detail — but only if you're viewing on a screen big enough to show them and sitting close enough to see them. On a 6-inch phone screen at arm's length, your eye can't distinguish 1080p from 4K. On a 65-inch TV across the room, you can.

Framerate: How Smooth the Motion Is

Framerate is how many frames per second (fps) the video shows. Common options:

  • 24 fps — cinema standard. Looks filmic and slightly soft on motion.
  • 30 fps — TV and most online video. Natural and balanced.
  • 60 fps — smooth motion. Sports, gameplay, fast camera movement.
  • 120 / 240 fps — slow-motion capture. Played back at normal speed for buttery slowmo.

Higher framerate means more frames to encode, which means more data. Going from 30 fps to 60 fps at the same per-frame quality increases file size by about 50-70%. For most everyday footage, 30 fps is plenty — 60 fps shines only for motion-heavy content.

Bitrate: The Quality Lever

Bitrate is how much data each second of video gets. We cover this in depth in our bitrate explainer, but the key point: bitrate is what actually determines how clean the picture looks. Resolution sets the canvas; bitrate is how much paint you put on it.

This is why a 4K YouTube video can look worse than a 1080p Blu-ray — YouTube's 4K is streamed at maybe 20 Mbps, while a Blu-ray's 1080p runs at 35 Mbps. More pixels doesn't help if each pixel is poorly described.

How the Three Interact

The three numbers constrain each other. The encoder has a fixed bitrate budget per second; it has to spread that budget across all the pixels in all the frames. So:

  • More resolution = more pixels to encode = each pixel gets less bitrate = potential softness.
  • Higher framerate = more frames to encode = each frame gets less bitrate = potential artifacts in motion.
  • Higher bitrate = more headroom = better quality at any resolution/framerate combination.

This is why 4K 60 fps needs dramatically more bitrate than 1080p 30 fps to look good — about 5-6× more. iPhone 4K HDR 60 fps footage lands at 30-50 Mbps because it genuinely needs that much to look clean at those specs.

Which Matters Most for Quality?

Ranked by impact on perceived quality:

  1. Bitrate — the biggest lever. Below a certain bitrate, no resolution or framerate will save you.
  2. Resolution — matters if the screen is big enough to show it.
  3. Framerate — matters mostly for motion-heavy content.

This is also why bitrate is the right thing to compress first. Lowering bitrate 60-80% on most consumer video is invisible to the eye. Lowering resolution immediately makes the picture visibly softer. VidZip's approach — bitrate only, resolution preserved — gets the smallest files at the highest perceived quality.

Which Matters Most for File Size?

Ranked by impact on file size:

  1. Bitrate — file size is essentially bitrate × duration. This is the direct lever.
  2. Duration — longer video = bigger file. Obvious but often forgotten.
  3. Framerate — 60 fps adds ~50-70% over 30 fps at matched quality.
  4. Resolution — only matters insofar as it forces higher bitrate. A low-bitrate 4K file can be smaller than a high-bitrate 1080p file.

The counterintuitive takeaway: resolution is the least direct lever for file size. People think "4K = big file," but it's really "high bitrate = big file, and 4K usually ships with high bitrate." Compress the bitrate and the 4K file shrinks like any other.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between 4K and 1080p?

Resolution. 4K is 3840×2160 pixels (about 8.3 million per frame), 1080p is 1920×1080 (about 2 million). 4K has four times the pixel count. On a big screen up close, 4K looks sharper. On a phone screen at normal viewing distance, the difference is often invisible — but the 4K file is several times larger.

Is 60 fps better than 30 fps?

For motion, yes — 60 fps looks smoother, especially for sports, gameplay, and fast camera movement. For most everyday footage (talking head, scenery), 30 fps looks natural and cinematic. 60 fps also increases file size by about 50% over 30 fps at the same quality.

Does higher resolution always mean better quality?

No. Resolution only sets the number of pixels — bitrate determines how well those pixels are encoded. A 4K video starved of bitrate looks blocky and soft, while a 1080p video with generous bitrate looks crisp. Bitrate matters more than resolution for perceived quality.

What resolution should I shoot video at on iPhone?

For most everyday use — sharing, messaging, social media — 1080p at 30 fps is plenty and produces manageable file sizes. Reserve 4K for footage you plan to edit, watch on a big screen, or keep as an archive. Switch in Settings → Camera → Record Video.

What matters more for file size: resolution or bitrate?

Bitrate, by far. File size is essentially bitrate × duration, regardless of resolution. You can have a huge 4K file or a tiny 4K file depending on bitrate. Lowering bitrate is the most direct way to shrink a video; lowering resolution is a secondary lever.
