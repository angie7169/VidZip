How to Convert HEVC to MP4 on iPhone (2026)

Updated July 2026 · 8 min read · By VidZip Team

You took a video on your iPhone, sent it to a friend on Windows, and they got a black screen or a "format not supported" error. The culprit is almost always HEVC — the high-efficiency codec iPhones use by default. This guide explains why iPhone uses HEVC, where it breaks compatibility, and the fastest way to convert HEVC to universally playable MP4.

Why iPhone Uses HEVC

Since iOS 11, iPhone shoots video in HEVC (H.265) by default. The reason is simple: HEVC is roughly 40-50% more efficient than the older H.264 codec, meaning the same quality video takes about half the storage. With 4K HDR footage chewing through gigabytes, that efficiency matters.

The tradeoff is compatibility. HEVC is well-supported on Apple devices, modern Android, and recent Windows, but it's not universal. Older Windows PCs, many web browsers, some editing tools, and most legacy devices can't decode it — or require a paid extension. If you've ever had an iPhone video refuse to play on someone else's device, HEVC was likely the reason.

MP4 (H.264), by contrast, is the universal language of video. Every device, browser, and player made in the last 15 years understands it. Converting HEVC to MP4 trades some efficiency for guaranteed playability — usually worth it when sharing.

Method 2: Change Camera Settings for Future Videos

To avoid the HEVC problem for future recordings:

  1. Open Settings → Camera → Formats.
  2. Switch from "High Efficiency" to "Most Compatible."

New videos will record as H.264 MP4 (well, MOV with H.264) instead of HEVC. They'll be larger for the same quality, but they'll play anywhere without conversion.

This doesn't help for videos you've already shot, but it's a good setting to flip if you regularly share with Windows users or older devices.

Method 3: The "Automatic" Setting (Unreliable)

iOS has a halfway option: in Settings → Camera → Formats, choose "Automatic." iPhone shoots HEVC but tries to convert to H.264 when sharing to non-Apple destinations.

In practice this is flaky. It sometimes works for AirDrop to a Mac, but export to Files, third-party apps, or share sheets often still produce HEVC. If you really need MP4 output every time, don't rely on Automatic — convert explicitly.

Method 4: Play It Without Converting (VLC)

If you don't want to convert and just want to view an HEVC file on a device that doesn't support it natively, install VLC media player (free, on Windows/Mac/Android). VLC decodes HEVC out of the box. This solves the playback problem for you, but the file is still HEVC — anyone else you share with will hit the same issue.

For sharing, conversion is the better path.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does iPhone shoot in HEVC or MP4?

By default, recent iPhones (iOS 11+) capture video in HEVC (H.265) wrapped in a .mov container for efficiency. Some settings and sharing methods produce MP4 (H.264) instead, but most camera footage you find in Photos is HEVC. This is why videos sometimes won't play on Windows PCs or older devices.

How do I convert HEVC to MP4 on iPhone?

The fastest way is to use VidZip: open the app, select the HEVC video, tap Compress. VidZip always outputs MP4 (H.264), so the result plays on any device. The conversion happens on your phone, no upload required.

Why won't my iPhone video play on Windows?

Because it's HEVC (H.265), and Windows doesn't include a free HEVC decoder. You either need to install the paid HEVC Video Extensions from the Microsoft Store, use a third-party player like VLC, or convert the video to MP4 (H.264), which Windows plays natively.

Can iPhone convert HEVC to MP4 automatically when sharing?

Yes, partially. In Settings → Camera → Formats, "Automatic" will try to share as H.264 when it detects a non-Apple destination (like AirDrop to a Windows PC). But this doesn't always trigger reliably, and many export paths (Files, third-party apps) still produce HEVC. Converting explicitly with a tool like VidZip is more reliable.

Does converting HEVC to MP4 reduce quality?

Re-encoding always slightly degrades quality in theory, but if you keep the bitrate roughly the same, the difference is invisible. H.264 is less efficient than HEVC, so an MP4 at the same quality will be somewhat larger. VidZip balances this by lowering bitrate to compensate, keeping size similar while ensuring universal playback.
